Green Turtle Coastal areas of Sri Lanka

The Green Turtle (Chelonia mydas) is the most common turtle found in Sri Lanka. They can grow up to 120 centimeters and weigh up to 180 kilograms. They are characterized by their smooth olive-colored shells, a small head, and four flippers that are perfectly adapted for swimming. Green Turtles are herbivores and primarily feed on seagrasses, algae, and other marine plants. They are also known to migrate thousands of kilometers between their nesting and feeding grounds, making them an important component of the marine ecosystem.

The Olive Ridley turtle is a smaller species of turtle that is known for its unique nesting behavior, where thousands of turtles come ashore to nest at the same time. The Olive Ridley Turtle (Lepidochelys olivacea) is one of the smallest sea turtles, growing up to 70 centimeters and weighing up to 50 kilograms. They are known for their distinctive heart-shaped shell and have a unique behavior of mass nesting, known as an "arribada". During this process, thousands of females come ashore simultaneously to lay their eggs, which provides a natural defense mechanism against predators. Olive Ridley Turtles are omnivores, feeding on a variety of marine animals including crabs, shrimp, and jellyfish.

Loggerhead turtles are one of the seven species of sea turtles found in the world, and they are commonly sighted in the coastal waters of Sri Lanka. These large-bodied turtles are known for their powerful jaws, large head, and reddish-brown carapace, which sets them apart from other sea turtles. Loggerheads have a unique life cycle and they spend most of their lives in the open ocean, where they feed on a variety of marine organisms such as jellyfish, crabs, and squid. During the breeding season, female loggerheads come ashore to nest on sandy beaches, digging nests to lay their eggs. These nests can hold up to 100 eggs, and the eggs typically hatch after about 60 days. Loggerhead turtles play an important role in the marine ecosystem, as they help maintain the balance of the food chain by controlling populations of jellyfish, their primary prey. Additionally, loggerhead turtles are also important indicators of the health of the marine environment. Their presence or absence can signal changes in the ocean's temperature, quality of water, and other ecological factors. Conservation efforts are ongoing in Sri Lanka to protect these amazing creatures and ensure their survival for future generations.
